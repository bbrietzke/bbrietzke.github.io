There are a number of different recipes for Ginger Beer out there, and I'm adding
another one to the mix.  It's not right, or wrong it's just the recipe I use.

This is what my family and I enjoy.  It's somewhat tweaked for what we have
available and what we like.  It's not super fizzy, but it is fizzy enough for us.  

I would drink this stuff all the time if I could get away with it.  *So* much tastier
than a commercial Ginger Ale or what-have-you.

## Ingredients
### Required
- 1/4lb Fresh Ginger ( organic if you can )
- 2 cups sugar
- 2 large lemons
- 1 gallon Water
### Optional
- 2 bags of tea ( i prefer oolong tea )
- 1 cup sugar

## Equipment
- micro-planer or zester
- 1 eight ounce or larger cup or bowl.
- 1 quart or larger pot or vessel
- 1 gallon or larger fermenting vessel w/airlock
- auto-siphon
- silicon stirring device
- up to 2 cups sugar


## Procedure
### Brewing
First off, make sure everything is clean.  I mean *clean*.  Sanitize the fermenting
vessel, stiring device and auto-siphon and let them drip dry while we do the rest.

In the eight-ounce cup/bowl, add approximately four ounces of the water, a teaspoon
or so of sugar and the yeast.

Process the ginger down to a small size.  I generally do 1/4 inch cubes. If you are
feeling mean or have some energy to work out, feel free to grate the ginger You do
not need to peel the ginger prior to processing.

Zest the two lemons. Cut the lemons in half and juice them into the fermenting vessel

Take the 1 quart pot/vessel ( after it has been cleaned ) and add 2 cups of water
from the 1 gallon of Water.  Once it is boiling, add the 2 cups of sugar to the water
and mix until it has completely dissolved.  

Kill the heat from the pot/vessel and add the processed ginger and the optional tea
to the hot water water and let the mixture steep for at least 15 minutes.  You can
wait till the mixture is cooled to room temperature if you choose.  

While the ginger/sugar/tea mixture is steeping, full the 1 gallon fermenting vessel
 with the remaining water ( don't make a mess ).   

Once the ginger/sugar/tea mixture has finished steeping, add all of that mixture
to the fermenting vessel and stir vigorously.  This will integrate the mixture with
the rest of the water and add oxygen.  

Add the cup of water/yeast to the fermenting vessel and stir vigorously again.

If you are adding additional sugars to the mixture, please add them now and stir
vigorously to integrate everything.

Seal the fermenting vessel and store it in a dark location that you can get to easily.

For the next five to seven days, check on the mixture at least once a day.  You
will want to make sure the airlock is bubbling and nothing had exploded.

### Bottling
After the beer has stopped bubbling, you may begin bottling.  

What you bottle the brew into is up to you.  Regular 12 ounce bottles are available
as well as 16 ounce flip top bottles ( my preference ).  Honestly anything will
work as long as you can make it airtight.

Before you begin bottling, make sure the bottles ( and caps ) are clean and sanitized along
with you auto-siphon and bottling wand.

Add 1/4 teaspoon sugar per 8 ounces of volume to each of your bottles ( i.e. 12 ounce
  bottles receive half a teaspoon, while 16 ounce bottles receive 3/4 teaspoon ).  
This is the priming sugar that will create bubbles!

You *can* add more priming sugar to each bottle, but it will increase the risk of
the bottle exploding.

Fill each of the bottles so that there is about one inch of head room left in each
bottle.  Seal the bottles and let them rest at room temperature in a dark location
for two to five days.

Then put the bottles in the refrigerator overnight.

From that point on, pull one out, have a taste and enjoy the fruits of your labors.
